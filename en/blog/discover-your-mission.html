Discover Your Mission: Write Your Story and Name the Essential

Reading time: ~ 5 min

Contents

  • Definitions
  • What is the mission?
  • Discover your mission

DEFINITIONS

Leadership = a call to action
Mission = mittere (to send) = being sent to do something
Vocation = vocare (to call) = being called to be someone

1. What is the Mission?

Your mission answers “who am I?”—your specific contribution to the common good.
To see it clearly, recognize your talents, distinctive capacities for action, and the stories you inherit (parents, grandparents). The more specific your life becomes to you, the easier it is to see your mission.
Vocation is the privileged relationship between the Divine and the self; it frames the mission.
Mission is what I do continuously because it is who I am. It guides my objectives.

“There is in many lives a mystical meaning, but few grasp it properly. This meaning is often conveyed to us in a coded way and, unable to decipher it, we despair at how meaningless life seems. The success of great lives often lies in deciphering the code that has been sent to us, understanding it, and learning to walk the straight path.” — Viatcheslav Ivanov

2. Discover your mission

Writing your story lets core ideas, lessons, and lived experiences emerge. Telling yourself your story—and contemplating it—reveals who you are.

How to write your story?

  • People who left an indelible mark on me;
  • Books and films that spark a thirst to build;
  • My talents;
  • My temperament;
  • My values;
  • Key events in my life;
  • Decisive choices I made;
  • Ideas I’m willing to sacrifice for;
  • My country—its greatness and its hardships.

Begin to write in gratitude and contemplation. Return to the text and amplify as memories surface.

My story is not only mine. It also belongs to those who, in one way or another, live in me because their influence was immense.

Remember what each person offered you along the way.
Condense your mission into 4–5 words—poetic, symbolic, short enough to remember, large enough to live.

Be subjective yet speak a universal language. Abstractions help reveal the thread that unifies everything: the essence of your mission.

QUESTIONS

  • What did my parents/grandparents give me?
  • What did my siblings/friends give me?
  • What did strangers give me?

A. My talent

To know one’s talents, experiment widely—and listen to people who reflect them back to you.

QUESTIONS

  • What do people consistently say about me?

B. My temperament

Temperament is also a gift.

Melancholic: break intellectual status quo and forge new ideas; contemplative, moved by concepts; often in the arts.

Choleric: launch ambitious projects for the common good; action‑oriented (politics, management).

Sanguine: gather people around a project—even one that isn’t your own.

Phlegmatic: bring rationality so a project is viable over time; scientific, methodical, passionate.

Which cultural or social challenge do I answer with passion and determination?

Your mission should be:
LARGE—so you can live it in many ways;
PRECISE—so you remember it always.

Some mission examples:

“To be the memory of a people who suffered an unspeakable tragedy.” — Alexandre Solzhenitsyn
“To defend scientific truth and the great moral truth that flows from it.” — Jérôme Lejeune
“To be a mother to the poorest of the poor on Earth.” — Mother Teresa
